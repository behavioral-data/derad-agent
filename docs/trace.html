Adam Smith witnessed one of the most profound reorganisations of human capital in history: the transition to mechanised production. Contemporaneous sources warned new machinery would deprive workers of the opportunity of getting a livelihood. Fears of mass unemployment accompanied technological progress then just as they do now.

Today it is AI that has revived these concerns. Some policymakers frame UBI as a necessary response to looming technological unemployment.

One study estimates providing an annual UBI of just over £11,000 per person in Britain would require a flat income tax rate of around 45%.

Advocates argue AI changes this calculus, making UBI financially feasible if AGI dramatically increases output without a corresponding increase in human work. The tax base could shift away from labour towards returns on capital and highly productive AI systems themselves.

Even on these optimistic assumptions, UBI would require the wholesale replacement of the existing welfare system — a far more radical and risky change than advocates acknowledge.
