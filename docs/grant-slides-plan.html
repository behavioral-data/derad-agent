Slide 02

The problem & why it matters now

Show on slideConceptual
  • Polarizing misinformation spreads fast on social media.
  • Fact-checks frequently don't land — and can backfire, hardening views and raising hostility.
  • Platforms are already deploying AI / crowd corrections (Community Notes, the @grok "fact-check me" phenomenon) — at scale, with little evidence on what works.
  • The gap: we study what corrections say; we know almost nothing about how their tone shapes acceptance and hostility.
Say

Frame the timeliness: AI fact-checking is being rolled out to millions right now without an evidence base for design choices. This study supplies one.

Leave out
No tooling, no models, no pipeline. This is a "why should you care" slide.

Slide 03

Research question & hypothesis

The intellectual core. Spend time here.

Show on slideConceptual — key definitions
  • RQ: How does the tone of an AI fact-check affect (1) engagement with the fact-check and (2) affective polarization among those exposed to it?
  • One crucial distinction (put it on the slide as two short definitions):
    Antagonistic polarization — treating the other side as an enemy to defeat (support for hostility, exclusion, even violence). The harmful kind.
    Agonistic polarization — vigorous disagreement that still treats opponents as legitimate. The healthy kind.
  • Hypothesis: satirical fact-checks will decrease antagonistic polarization and earn higher engagement than agreeable or neutral ones; their effect on agonistic polarization is an open question.
Say: the bet is counterintuitive — "edgy/satirical" sounds like it should inflame, but the theory (Mouffe's agonism) predicts satire can defuse the enemy-making kind of polarization while keeping people engaged. That tension is what makes the study interesting. Cite Mouffe (2000), Stray et al. (2026) in one line; don't belabor the literature.

Slide 04

The core idea: hold the facts constant, vary only the tone

Your methodological selling point.

Show on slideConceptual
  • One bot. Every claim is verified through the same evidence pipeline.
  • The only thing that differs between conditions is the tone of the final reply.
  • Three tones: Agreeable ☺️ · Neutral 🧐 · Satirical (Edgy) 😎.
  • So any difference in outcomes is attributable to tone — not to different facts, sources, or accuracy.
Suggested visual

A simple fork: one post → [ verify against evidence ] → 3 reply bubbles, each a different tone but showing the same facts & sources. Label the shared trunk "identical for everyone" and the fork "the only thing we change."

Say
This is the experimental control, in plain words. It's what separates this from a demo and makes it a study.
Leave out
Don't say "invariance boundary," "frozen object," or name pipeline stages. The plain-English version above is stronger for this room.

Slide 05

How it works — the participant's view

Show on slideLight technical

A 4-step flow:

  • 1 A participant finds a post they disagree with and tags @eddie_bot in a reply.
  • 2 The bot reads the post (and any image) and figures out what kind of response it needs.
  • 3 It verifies against live web evidence and writes a reply in that participant's assigned tone.
  • 4 It posts the reply with a link to a full evidence page (sources + reasoning).
Say

Keep it to the user-visible journey. The point is that it's a real, autonomous agent acting on real posts in real time — not a mock-up.

Leave out
No streaming/queueing, no cloud, no per-stage breakdown. One clean diagram.

Slide 06

It does more than "true / false"

Show on slideConceptual — name the 5

The bot picks the right kind of response for each post:

Verify a checkable factual claim.
Add context when a claim is technically true but misleadingly framed.
Challenge a strong opinion with credible counter-evidence.
Surface perspectives on a genuinely contested topic.
Decline when there's nothing to engage (a joke, pure taste).
Say: real posts aren't all checkable facts. A blunt true/false bot would be wrong-footed constantly and feel preachy. This calibration makes the intervention realistic and defensible — and it's the same across all three tones.

Slide 07

Why the fact-checks are trustworthy

Pre-empts the obvious "but does the AI just make things up?" concern.

Show on slideLight technical
  • Grounded in real, current web evidence — it reads actual articles, not its own memory.
  • Weighs source quality — fact-checkers and reputable news count for more than unknown blogs.
  • Conservative by design — when evidence is thin it says "not enough evidence" rather than guess.
  • Every reply links to an auditable evidence page with the sources and reasoning.
Say

Funders worry about AI hallucination. The honest message: accuracy is held to a deliberately cautious bar, it's transparent, and — critically — accuracy is identical across tones, so it can't confound the result.

Leave out
No source-tier table internals, no model names, no retrieval mechanics.

Slide 08

The three tones, side by side

The payoff slide — make the manipulation concrete.

Show on slideWorked example

Take one real, easily-grasped case (a miscaptioned viral photo works well) and show the three replies stacked, with the shared facts/sources highlighted identically:

Agreeable ☺️
Acknowledges why someone would believe it, then gently corrects with the same sources.
Neutral 🧐
Plain, declarative correction; names the same sources; no flourish.
Satirical 😎
Pokes fun at the claim and its source — never the person — while landing the same facts.
Say: same facts, same sources, same verdict — only the voice changes. Call out the satire boundary explicitly here: it targets the claim and the source, never the individual. That single sentence reassures the ethics-minded in the room and sets up slide 11.

Slide 09

Study design

Show on slideConceptual
  • Field experiment on X with the live bot.
  • ~30 UW students recruited; each assigned one tone at enrolment (between-subjects; tone stays fixed per person).
  • Over 5 days, each finds 10 high-engagement posts they disagree with and invokes the bot on them.
  • Plus organic bystanders — anyone who sees a reply — who get a randomly-assigned tone.
Say

Two exposed populations: the recruited invokers (measured directly via survey) and the bystanders (measured via public engagement). Note this gives both controlled self-report and real-world behavioral signal.

Suggested visual
A small 5-day timeline, or a two-box "invokers / bystanders" split.

Slide 11

Ethics & safety

Funders weight this heavily — give it a full slide.

Show on slideConceptual — thorough
  • Disclosed research bot — it identifies itself as one.
  • Opt-out registry + deletion on request.
  • Post-hoc debriefing for identifiable users.
  • Satire boundary — targets the claim and source, never the person; no slurs or identity attacks (enforced in the system, not just policy).
  • Stopping rule — halt the satirical arm if downstream hostility rises.
  • IRB approval + preregistration before launch.
Say

You've thought hard about the risk of deploying an "edgy" bot on real people. The boundary and stopping rule are concrete, built-in safeguards — not aspirations. This is where you build trust.
